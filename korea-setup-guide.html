Setting up in Korea, without the guesswork.

The practical basics a foreign company needs before signing a Korean lease: entity options, incentives and how leasing here actually works.

Three ways to have a presence in Korea

Most foreign companies choose one of three structures, in increasing order of commitment:

  • Liaison office. The lightest option. It can conduct market research and support functions but cannot generate revenue in Korea. Registration is with a Korean tax office.
  • Branch office. A registered extension of the foreign parent that can conduct revenue-generating business. Common for service and support operations such as field engineering teams.
  • Subsidiary (local corporation). A separate Korean legal entity under the Foreign Investment Promotion Act. The standard route when hiring at scale or applying for FDI incentives.

All three can sign a commercial lease. Which one fits depends on your revenue model and headcount plan, and your tax adviser should make the final call.

Incentives worth knowing about

Korea actively courts foreign investment in advanced industries. Two things are worth checking before you budget:

  • Cash grants. Foreign investors in national advanced strategic technologies, semiconductors included, may qualify for cash grants of up to 50% of the investment amount, subject to review.
  • Local support. Regional governments, including North Chungcheong Province around the Cheongju semiconductor cluster, operate investment support desks and fast-track permitting for related businesses.

Invest Korea, the national investment promotion agency under KOTRA, is the standard first point of contact. We are happy to make the introduction.

How Korean commercial leases work

  • Deposit (bo-jeung-geum). Korean leases pair a monthly rent with a refundable deposit, typically around 10 months of rent for commercial space. It is returned at the end of the term.
  • Term. Commercial terms of 1 to 2 years are standard, and tenants enjoy statutory renewal protection for up to 10 years under the Commercial Building Lease Protection Act.
  • Maintenance fees. Buildings charge a monthly management fee for shared facilities, billed separately from rent. Ask for it in writing; ours is stated in the lease.
  • Fit-out. Interior works are normally the tenant's cost, but rent-free periods to cover fit-out time are a common and reasonable ask.
